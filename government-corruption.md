<h2 id="content">A Quick Dive into Money and Politics</h2>
<p>For those who might need a quick refresher on these terms: 'debt' refers to the accumulated loans that our government has taken out, and 'deficit' refers to the annual amount for which our government must take out additional loans each year.
  
  As mentioned before, our current situation is due to massive increases to our debt and deficit over a very short time; the past 10 years, to be exact. The reason it's now a massive emergency is because of a simple concept: interest. Interest on debt can be pretty minor, so long as the debt remains small. An interest rate of 2%, for instance, is nothing when you've taken out a personal loan of $1,000. That's just $20 each payment period. When debt gets orders of magnitude larger, though, the story changes altogether. A loan of $10,000 at the same 2% interest rate results in $200 each payment period. It should be obvious just how much more serious the two situations are.
  
  That's basically what's happened to us here in America. Suppose that, a decade ago, our debt used to be around $10,000 at a 2% interest rate, and our Congress had dedicated $200 of its $1,000 budget each year to handle it. Today, our debt is effectively around $18,000 at that same 2% interest rate, and we suddenly have to allocate $360 of our $1,000 annual budget to interest alone. Given that Congress hasn't legislated any new revenue sources and that Congress was already spending well over their budget, this worsens the problem substantially.
  
  There are very clearly two sets of actions needed to keep our country from defaulting on its debt: cutting costs and raising revenues. Looking closely at this issue, here is what I've noticed so far (most of this data comes from the IRS' own data).</p>
<div class="row">
	<div class="6u 12u$(small)">
		<h3>We Can Still Salvage This</h3>
		<p>Our federal debt is at $37 trillion. Our deficit is at $1.8 trillion. Out of the $14.7 trillion in income earned by taxpayers, the IRS collected $2.2 trillion of that as revenue. Furthermore, the total value of all wealth held in America sits at around $160 trillion. Put simply: we have a number of ways we can remedy this situation before it gets truly out of hand.</p>
	</div>
	<div class="6u$ 12u$(small)">
		<h3>Reducing Debt Also Reduces Deficit</h3>
		<p>As illustrated by the example above, the actual total amount of debt also influences how quickly it grows. Currently, the interest accrued on our debt is $1.1 trillion every year. If we were to cut our total debt in half, that would also cut the interest gains in half. This, in turn, reduces how much funding we need to cut for vital programs that help the vast majority of Americans.</p>
	</div>
	<!-- Break -->
	<div class="4u 12u$(medium)">
		<h3>Large Budget Changes Are to Blame</h3>
		<p>The reality is, our debt didn't balloon out of control because of comparatively tiny expenditures. The cause of our current problem is right there in our nation's revenue and spending history. The 2017 Tax Cuts act contributed $450 billion to our deficit. Excessive payments to Private Military Contractors (who earn between 2 to 4 times as much as actual military service members) contributes another $300 to $450 billion to our deficit. These are among the largest offenders that have arisen in recent years.</p>
